Dr. Jose Rizal

Nationalist • Writer • Polymath

José Rizal, born in 1861, was a Filipino nationalist who symbolized resistance against Spanish colonial rule. His education, initially under private tutors and later in Europe, fueled his passion for justice and learning. Through essays and novels like "Noli Me Tangere" and "El Filibusterismo," he exposed colonial abuses and inspired Filipino activism. Despite his peaceful intentions, he was executed in 1896 at age 35 for sedition. Rizal's martyrdom ignited the Filipino quest for independence, and his legacy of patriotism and social justice endures today.

Family

Francisco Mercado (1818-1898)

José Rizal's father, was a wealthy landowner, farmer, and merchant. He hailed from Binan, Laguna.

Teodora Alonso Realonda (1827-1911)

José Rizal's mother, was a highly educated woman who was skilled in various languages and arts. She was from Sta. Cruz, Manila.

Saturnina Rizal (1850-1913)

José Rizal's eldest sister, who became a teacher and later married Manuel T. Hidalgo.

Paciano Rizal (1851-1930)

José Rizal's older brother, who greatly influenced him. He was a supporter of his brother's nationalist ideas and was involved in revolutionary activities.

Narcisa Rizal (1852-1939)

José Rizal's sister, who was known for her strong personality and for being a caregiver to her siblings.

Olympia Rizal (1855-1887)

José Rizal's sister, who died at a young age.

Lucia Rizal (1857-1919)

José Rizal's sister, who became a teacher and later married Máximo Herbosa.

Maria Rizal (1859-1945)

Philosophy

Educational Philosophy

Value of Education: Rizal believed in the transformative power of education. He viewed education not only as a means to acquire knowledge and skills but also as a tool for personal growth, enlightenment, and social progress.

Empowerment through Education: Rizal emphasized the importance of education in empowering individuals and communities. He believed that through education, people could gain the critical thinking skills, self-confidence, and knowledge necessary to assert their rights and advocate for social change.

Education for National Identity: Rizal saw education as crucial for the development of national identity and consciousness. He promoted the teaching of Filipino history, culture, and language as essential components of education, enabling Filipinos to understand their heritage and assert their distinct national identity.

Education for Moral and Ethical Development: Rizal advocated for an education system that not only imparted academic knowledge but also nurtured moral and ethical values. He believed that education should cultivate virtues such as integrity, compassion, and social responsibility, shaping individuals into conscientious and ethical members of society.

Access to Education: Rizal was a proponent of universal education, advocating for equal access to quality education for all, regardless of social class or background. He believed that education should be accessible to everyone as a fundamental human right and a means to promote social equity and opportunity.

Religious Philosophy

Tolerance and Respect: Rizal espoused principles of religious tolerance and respect for diverse beliefs. He believed in the importance of mutual understanding and harmony among people of different religious backgrounds, rejecting religious intolerance and bigotry.

Critical Thinking: Rizal encouraged critical examination of religious beliefs and practices. He emphasized the importance of rational inquiry and intellectual freedom in matters of faith, advocating for individuals to question and explore religious teachings rather than accepting them uncritically.

Spirituality and Morality: While Rizal was critical of certain aspects of organized religion, he maintained a deep sense of spirituality and morality. He believed in the importance of living a virtuous and ethical life, guided by principles of compassion, justice, and integrity, irrespective of religious affiliation.

Humanitarianism: Rizal's religious philosophy was characterized by a commitment to humanitarian values. He believed in the inherent dignity and worth of every human being and advocated for social justice, compassion, and solidarity with the less fortunate as essential expressions of religious faith.

Political Philosophy

Nationalism: Rizal was a staunch nationalist who advocated for the recognition of the Filipino identity and the assertion of Filipino rights against colonial oppression.

Reformism: While critical of the injustices perpetrated by the Spanish colonial regime, Rizal promoted peaceful reform through education, legal means, and engagement with the ruling authorities.

Liberty and Democracy: Rizal believed in the principles of liberty, equality, and democracy, advocating for the establishment of a just and democratic society where Filipinos would enjoy equal rights and opportunities.

Ethical/Moral Philosophy

Human Dignity: Rizal emphasized the inherent dignity and worth of every individual, regardless of race or social status. He condemned the dehumanizing effects of colonialism and championed the dignity and rights of all people.

Integrity and Virtue: Rizal upheld the importance of personal integrity and moral virtue. He believed in the power of ethical behavior and personal responsibility to effect positive change in society.

Compassion and Justice: Rizal advocated for compassion towards the marginalized and oppressed, urging individuals to uphold principles of justice and fairness in their interactions with others.

Social Philosophy

Education: Rizal viewed education as a fundamental tool for social progress and enlightenment. He emphasized the importance of education in fostering critical thinking, national consciousness, and social reform.

Social Justice: Rizal was deeply concerned with social inequality and injustice, particularly the exploitation and discrimination faced by the Filipino people under colonial rule. He called for greater social equity and the alleviation of poverty and suffering.

Civic Engagement: Rizal encouraged active participation in civic life and social responsibility. He believed in the collective responsibility of citizens to contribute to the welfare and advancement of society.

Education

Early Childhood Education (1861-1872):

Jose Rizal was born on June 19, 1861, in Calamba, Laguna, Philippines. He was the seventh of eleven children of Francisco Mercado and Teodora Alonso. His parents recognized his intellectual potential early on and provided him with basic education at home. His mother, Teodora, who was highly educated herself, played a significant role in his early education, teaching him reading, writing, and arithmetic.

Formal Education at Ateneo Municipal de Manila (1872-1877):

At the age of 11, Rizal was sent to Manila to pursue formal education at the Ateneo Municipal de Manila, one of the most prestigious schools in the Philippines at the time. He studied under the Jesuit order and excelled academically, particularly in literature, art, and languages. Rizal's time at Ateneo helped shape his critical thinking skills and instilled in him a sense of social justice.

University Education in Europe (1878-1882):

In 1878, at the age of 17, Rizal traveled to Europe to pursue higher education, following the wishes of his parents who wanted him to become a doctor. He enrolled at the Universidad Central de Madrid in Spain, where he pursued a degree in Medicine and also took courses in philosophy and letters. During his time in Europe, Rizal's exposure to the intellectual and political climate of the continent deeply influenced his nationalist sentiments and advocacy for reforms in the Philippines.

Further Studies in Europe and Expanding Horizons (1882-1887):

Rizal continued his studies in Europe, moving to Paris, Heidelberg, and then to Berlin. He continued his medical studies, specializing in ophthalmology, while also immersing himself in the cultural, social, and political milieu of Europe. He became fluent in several languages, including Spanish, French, German, and English. His experiences in Europe broadened his perspectives on nationalism, colonialism, and social justice, shaping his views on the Philippines' plight under Spanish colonial rule.

Return to the Philippines and Political Activism (1887-1892):

After completing his studies, Rizal returned to the Philippines in 1887. He became actively involved in advocating for reforms and social change through his writings and political activities. His novels, "Noli Me Tangere" and "El Filibusterismo," which exposed the injustices of Spanish colonial rule, sparked nationalistic fervor and played a crucial role in the Philippine Revolution.
